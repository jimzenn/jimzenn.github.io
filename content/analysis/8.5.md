{{% definition name="Compactness" status="" %}}

A metric space $(X,d)$ is said to be **compact** iff every sequence in $(X,d)$ has at least one convergent subsequence.

A subset $Y$ of a metric space $X$ is said to be **compact** iff the subspace $(Y,d|\_{Y\times Y})$ is compact.

{{% /definition %}}

{{% remarks name="" %}}

The notion of a set $Y$ being compact is <u>intrinsic</u>, in the sense that it only depends on the metric function $d|\_{(Y, d|\_{Y \times Y})}$ restricted to $Y$, and not on the choice of the ambient space $X$.

The notions of completeness and of boundedness are also intrinsic, but the notions of open and closed are not.

{{% /remarks %}}

{{% definition name="Bounded sets" status="" %}}

Let $(X,d)$ be a metric space, and let $Y$ be a subset of $X$. We say that $Y$ is **bounded** iff there exists a ball $B(x,r)$ in $X$ which contains $Y$.

{{% /definition %}}

{{% proposition name="" index="" status="" %}}

A compact metric space is both complete and bounded.

{{% /proposition %}}

{{% corollary name="Compact sets are closed and bounded" index="" %}}

Let $(X,d) $ be a metric space, and let $Y$ be a compact subset of $X$. Then $Y$ is closed and bounded.

{{% /corollary %}}

{{% theorem name="Heine-Borel Theorem" index="" status="" %}}

Let $(R^n, d) $ be a Euclidean space with either the Euclidean ($d\_{l^2}$) metric, the taxicab ($d\_{l^1}$) metric, or the sup-norm ($d\_{l^\infty} $) metric. Let $E$ be a subset of $\R^n $.

Then $E$ is compact if and only if it is closed and bounded.

{{% /theorem %}}

{{% theorem name="" index="" status="" %}}

Let $ f: [a, b] \to \R $ be a continuous function. Then $ f $ attains its max and min in $[a, b]$. max : $\exists c \in [a, b] $ such that $f( c ) \geq f(x) , \forall x \in [a, b]$

{{% /theorem %}}

{{% corollary name="" index="" %}}

Let $(X,d)$ be a metric space, and let $K\_1, K\_2, ...$ be a sequence of non-empty compact subsets of $X$ such that

$K\_1 \subset K\_2 \subset K\_3 \subset ... $

Then the intersection $\bigcap\_{ n=1 }^ \infty K\_n $ is non-empty.

{{% /corollary %}}

{{% theorem name="" index="" status="" %}}

Let $(X,d) $ be a metric space.

1. If $Y$ is a compact subset of $X$, and $Z \subseteq Y $, then $Z $ is compact if and only if $Z$ is closed.

2. If $Y\_1, \_2, ..., \_n $ are a finite collection of compact subsets of $X$, then their union $Y\_1 \cup Y\_2 \cup ... \cup Y\_n $ is also compact.

3. Every finite subset of $X$  (including the empty set) is compact.

{{% /theorem %}}
