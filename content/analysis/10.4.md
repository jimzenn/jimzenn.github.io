{{% note name="Different Types of Limits" %}}

1. **(limits of sequences of points in a metric space)** <br> $\limu{ n }{ \infty } x^{(n)} $
2. **(limiting values of functions at a point)** <br> $\limd{ x }{ x\_0 }{ x \in E } f(x) $
3. **(pointwise limits of sequences of functions)** <br> $f^{(n)}$
4. **(uniform limits of functions)** <br> $f^{(n)} $

Notice that (4) can be viewed as a special case of (1).

{{% /note %}}

{{% definition name="Metric spaces of bounded functions" status="" %}}

Suppose $(X, d\_X) $ and $(Y, d\_Y) $ are metric spaces. We let $B(X \to Y) $ denote the space of bounded functions from $X$ to $Y$:

$$B(X \to Y) \triangleq \set{ f | f: X \to Y\text{ is a bounded function}} $$

We define a metric $d\_ \infty : B(X \to Y) \times B(X \to Y) \to \R^+$ as 

$$d\_ \infty (f, g) \triangleq \supr\_{x \in X}{ d\_Y(f(x), g(x))}$$

for all $f, g \in B(X \to Y)$.

The metric is known as the **sup norm metric** or the **$L^ \infty $ metric**.

We will also use $d\_{B(X \to Y)} $ as a synonym for $d\_ \infty $.

{{% /definition %}}

{{% note name="$\none{}d\_\infty(f,g)$ is always finite" %}}

The distance $d\_\infty(f,g)$ is always finite in $B(X \to Y) $.


{{% proof index="" method="" %}}

$f, g \in B(X \to Y)$

Then,

$\exists y\_0, y\_1 \in Y, r\_1, r\_2 \in \R, \forall x \in X,$

$$f(x) \in B(y\_0, r\_1), g(x) \in B(y\_1, r\_2)$$

That is

$$d\_Y(f(x), y\_0) < r\_1, d\_Y(g(x), y\_1) < r\_2$$

Then

$$\begin{align\*}
d\_Y(f(x), g(x)) &\leq d\_Y(f(x), y\_0) + d\_Y(y\_0, y\_1) + d\_Y(y\_1, g(x)) \br
&= r\_1 + r\_2 + d\_Y(y\_1, y\_2) < \infty
\end{align\*}$$

Hence

$$d\_ \infty(f,g) = \supr\_{x \in X}{ d\_Y(f(x), g(x))} < \infty$$

{{% /proof %}}

{{% /note %}}

{{% proposition name="" index="" status="" %}}

$(X, d\_X), (Y, d\_Y)$. Let $(f^{(n)})\_{n =1}^ \infty \subseteq B(X \to Y) $, and let $f$ be another function in $B(X \to Y)$. Then,

$(f^{(n)})^\infty\_{ n =1}$ converges to $f$ in the metric $d\_{B(X \to Y)}$ if and only if $(f^{(n)})^\infty\_{n =1}$ converges uniformly to $f$.

{{% /proposition %}}

{{% theorem name="The space of continuous functions is complete" index="" status="" %}}

$(X, d\_X), (Y, d\_Y)$. $(Y, d\_Y)$ complete. The space $(C(X \to Y), d\_{B(X \to Y)}|\_{C(X \to Y) \times (X \to Y)})$ is a complete subspace of $(B(X \to Y), d\_{B(X \to Y)}) $. In other words, every Cauchy sequence of functions in $C(X \to Y) $ converges to a function in $C(X \to Y) $.


{{% /theorem %}}
